First World Problems - Asia Edition

2011-03-16
photo via the Daily Mail
Needless to say (but, like anyone who opens a sentence with this phrase, I will go ahead and say it anyway), there are legitimate and catastrophic problems in Asia right now - from ongoing human rights abuses in China to the recent disasters in Japan.  This post is not about those.  I'm a humor writer first and foremost, and my focus groups reveal that it's "too soon" to go after those ripe targets with my typically acerbic wit.

This post is about some of the other problems, originating in Asia, that are hitting the First World in endless tsunami-like waves of...that's not the best way to put it...that are shaking life in the developed world down to a pile of rubble...no, still not it...that are really inconveniencing wealthy Americans an awful, awful lot.


First, a super-crazy Chinese coal magnate has just paid over a million dollars for a dog.  The dog's name in Chinese is "Hong Dong," which I can only assume will provoke a lot less schoolyard jokes in his native land than they would here in the US.  From the article, we get this brilliant insight:

The high price paid for the dog is a sign that the red Tibetan mastiff has become a status symbol in China, replacing jewellery and cars as a way for the super-rich to show off their wealth.
I'd quibble with the word replacing in that sentence, as I'm sure jewelry and cars will always have a special place in the hearts of the super-rich; but other than that - an astute observation.  What the article does not comment on, however, is the First World Arms Race that this purchase has no-doubt set off.  I mean, it used to be so easy for an American or European to prove he had more money than he could possibly know what to do with.  If he's going to have to start breeding a stable of 280-lb millionaire dogs just to keep up, then it's a whole new ballgame.

Now, closer to home, we get this:

Bay Area Sushi Bars Face Daunting Supply Shortages

Again, I'd quibble with the wording here.  A massive wave of ocean water rushing at your entire seaside town is "daunting."  Sitting in your penthouse apartment atop a 50-story building during a record-breaking earthquake is "daunting."  Living in the Bay Area and having to eat something other than sushi for the foreseeable future is, at best, a mild inconvenience.  The entire article linked above could have been produced by a reasonably intelligent third grader as a creative writing assignment:  "In 150 words or less, tell me what you think would happen to the sushi industry in America if the place where lots of fish come from suffered a crippling natural disaster."  But the kicker is the closing paragraph, which I assume was written by an adult in the "news" business, and edited by a colleague with similar credentials:
It’s thought that what product is available in Japan will be saved for restaurants in that country, and restaurants and distributors in the United States could see lower quality seafood being shipped overseas.
I have neither the time nor the inclination to watch the video they embedded, to see what kind of hard-hitting exposé they managed to cobble together out of this material.
